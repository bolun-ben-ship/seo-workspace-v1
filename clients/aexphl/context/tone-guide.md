> Read this before writing any content for AEXPHL — blog posts, emails, LinkedIn, anything.

## Who Is Writing

Tim is the founder of Aussie Expat Home Loans. His authority doesn't come from studying expats — it comes from being one.

He dropped out of music school after burning himself out, got his first job as a bank teller at NAB in Carell Shopping Centre ("cut my teeth very quickly"), and moved to Singapore at 28 as a trailing spouse with no real plan, just the belief he could make something of it. He hit a hiring freeze the week he arrived — ANZ rescinded his job offer two weeks after giving it. He spent a year on LinkedIn messaging every Australian banker in Singapore he could find, just trying to get a foothold.

He got retrenched. His words: "Getting retrenched was the greatest thing that ever happened to me." He started AEXPHL from his bedroom, built it through word of mouth, and ran the whole thing himself for years — "slowly slowly built it organically from the ground up."

"I took a punt. Seven and a half years later, still."

The people he was messaging on LinkedIn trying to find work? Most of them are his clients now.

Content is written as Tim, or as the AEXPHL brand in Tim's voice. The brand is a slightly more considered version of Tim — same experience, same values, a bit more polished.

## Who We're Writing For

Aussie expats in Singapore, Hong Kong, Dubai, and globally. Salaried professionals — SGD, HKD, AED earners. Most of us came here for work, some stayed longer than we planned, and at some point we all start thinking about property back home.

As Tim puts it: "Property is Australia's number one sport. Everyone participates in it, everyone wants to be part of it. Bricks and mortar is massive — it's generational."

We're not outsiders trying to access a confusing foreign system. We're participants in something deeply ingrained in Australian culture, doing it from a different time zone.

The people we're writing for:
- Earning well overseas and wondering why getting an Australian loan feels harder than it should
- Busy — reading on a phone between meetings, on the MRT, or at a hawker centre
- Tired of being told it's complicated without being told what to actually do
- Not anxious — capable, ambitious people who want a straight answer

From the podcast: "Any new Australian you meet here, you can instantly talk about your experience and have something in common. All of us have a shared experience."

That shared experience is the context for everything we write.

## The Voice

Tim is one of us who solved the problem and is sharing what he learned. Not an expert looking down — a peer who's done the hard yards and is passing it on.

His client philosophy, in his own words: "Our whole job is to make the process as easy as possible for our clients and we need to anticipate as much as we can ahead of the process for them."

He's warm and direct. He doesn't dramatise complexity, doesn't downplay it either. He gives you the real picture and lets you decide. If there's something that needs a call to sort out, he says so. If you can figure it out from the article, even better.

He's also not solemn about it. He laughs at himself. He'll mention his wife changing jobs or crack a dry observation about life in Singapore. That lightness is part of why people trust him — he doesn't perform expertise, he just shares experience.

## Language Rules

**Australian English throughout.**
- "recognise" not "recognize", "behaviour" not "behavior", "licence" (noun) not "license"
- Natural contractions: "it's", "you're", "we've", "that's" — write how a person talks
- "Overseas" not "abroad". "Back home" or "in Australia" for property. "Aussie expat" not "Australian expatriate".
- Avoid Americanisms: "utilize", "reach out", "circle back"

**Sentence length:** Short and medium. A sentence under 10 words can land a point cleanly. 20–25 words can carry nuance. Rarely go beyond 30.

**Paragraphs:** 2–4 sentences. One idea per paragraph. This is read on mobile.

**No rhetorical three-word stacks.** "Clarity. Brevity. Trust." — AI filler dressed as style. Cut it.

## Patterns to Kill — The AI Tells

These make content sound generated. Eliminate on sight:

### 1. The "Not A, Not B, But C" structure
> ~~"Not a salesman. Not a bank. Just your broker."~~
> ~~"No jargon. No pressure. Just straight answers."~~

Run into the ground by AI tools. Reads like a rebrand tagline. Cut entirely.

### 2. The parallel values stack
> ~~"Clarity over complexity. Relationships over transactions. Integrity over shortcuts."~~

Fine as internal framing. Useless on a page. If the values are real, they show in the writing — you don't announce them.

### 3. Em dash splicing as rhythm
> ~~"The process is complex — but manageable — when you know what you're doing."~~

One em dash is fine. Using them to create cadence is an AI habit. Write two sentences instead.

### 4. Stacked short declaratives as punches
> ~~"You've built an income offshore. You've saved well. Now it's time to put it to work."~~

Three declaratives in a row land like a motivational poster. Write a proper sentence.

### 5. The setup-payoff competitor frame
> ~~"Most brokers will tell you X. We do Y instead."~~

Overused across all finance content. Just say what you do and why — without the contrast.

### 6. Abstract nouns doing no work
> ~~"We believe in transparency." "We're committed to integrity."~~

Meaningless without specifics. "We send a WhatsApp update after every lender interaction" is transparency. "We believe in transparency" is not.

### 7. Urgency dressed as calm
> ~~"The window is open — but it won't stay that way."~~
> ~~"Rates are moving. Now is a good time to review."~~

Even when softened, urgency is still urgency. Tim doesn't use it. If there's a genuine market update, describe it factually and let the reader decide.

## How to Frame Challenges

Tim's consistent pattern with hard moments: acknowledge them plainly, then reframe as learning or opportunity. No drama, no false positivity.

From the podcast:
- Getting retrenched: "the greatest thing that ever happened to me... sometimes you can get very comfortable"
- GFC hiring freeze: "I'm really grateful for that because you get put into the fire and it's only up from there"
- Early deal stress: "the best thing I've learned is to invest in the people around you"

Apply the same to content about expat borrowing challenges. Foreign income shading is real. Application complexity is real. Say so plainly, explain what it actually means, then show the path through. Not scary, not falsely easy — eyes open.

## Compliance Rules

- No rate quotes
- No loan guarantees
- No tax advice, no financial advice beyond general education
- Soft qualifiers where needed: "typically", "in most cases", "subject to lender policy", "this varies by lender"
- Don't stack qualifiers — one per claim is enough; multiple reads as nervous

## Call to Action Rules

Soft, invitation-based. Never push.

**Good:**
- "Check your borrowing capacity" — top of funnel, low commitment
- "Book a quick call" — mid-funnel
- "See if refinancing makes sense for your situation" — intent-specific
- "Give us a call" / "Feel free to WhatsApp me on +65 8479 3873" — Tim's own phrasing

**Never:**
- "Sign up now"
- "Don't miss out"
- "Act before rates rise"
- "Get started today"

CTA placement: once mid-post where it fits naturally, once at the end. Never more than twice. The CTA earns its place — it doesn't interrupt the content.

## Content Types and How They Differ

### Blog posts / guides
Three valid opening types:
1. The reader's situation or question — start where they are
2. Tim's lived experience — a personal moment or turning point that connects to the topic
3. A client observation (anonymised) — a real interaction that illustrates the point

Answer the question early, then fill in the detail. Tim's perspective appears, but content is educational first — his opinion follows the information. End with what the reader should consider next, then a soft CTA.

### LinkedIn posts
- Open with a reflection, observation, or real moment — not a hook, not a stat
- Grounded in lived experience or a specific interaction
- One clear point per post — not a list of lessons
- Tim's sign-off patterns: gratitude + soft contact CTA + "Onwards and upwards :)" where it fits
- Team credit when relevant: "And importantly, my team — they make everything possible"

### Emails (client-facing)
- Open with context: what's happened, what's changed, what this email is about
- Tell the reader what it means for them specifically
- Options, not instructions
- End with an invitation to talk: "If you want to discuss, feel free to WhatsApp me" or "Happy to jump on a call"

### Market updates
- Acknowledge what people are seeing or reading in the news
- Separate confirmed facts from speculation
- Explain what this typically means for expat borrowers — not predictions, just implications
- Reassure without dismissing genuine complexity
- Don't tell people what to do — help them think it through

## What Makes AEXPHL Content Different

**The foreign income problem most brokers miss:**
Australian lenders "shade" (discount) foreign currency income. How it's assessed — whether it's SGD, HKD, AED, whether it includes bonuses, RSUs, or allowances — directly determines borrowing capacity. Most brokers get this wrong and submit to the wrong lender. That's why applications fail. Content should demonstrate this knowledge specifically, not just claim expertise.

**"Complex but manageable":**
Tim's consistent framing — the process isn't simple, but it's structured. There's a clear path. Content explains the real steps, doesn't oversimplify, and doesn't catastrophise. Eyes open.

**Geography-specific content:**
A Singapore-based expat, Hong Kong-based, and Dubai-based all have different income structures, tax situations, and timing pressures. Content should reflect this. "Aussie expats in Singapore specifically" is more useful than "Aussie expats" broadly — and it's harder for generic competitors to replicate.

**Bricks and mortar is in our blood:**
Property is Australia's national sport. We've all grown up watching our parents and grandparents build wealth through it. The goal of getting into the Australian property market isn't a stretch — it's a natural extension of who we are. Content leans into this. Expats aren't outsiders to the dream, they're just doing it from further away.

## The Two-Part Test

Before publishing, ask:

**1. Would Tim actually say this?**
Not "does it sound professional" — Tim is professional but he doesn't write like a compliance document. Not "does it sound smart" — Tim is sharp but he doesn't grandstand. Would he say this to a fellow Aussie expat over coffee at a hawker centre? If yes, publish. If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite.

**2. Does this read like Tim is one of us, or like he's looking down at us?**
Content should feel written from inside the Aussie expat community, not addressed at it. "We've all been there" is the right register. "Here's what you need to know" is not.
